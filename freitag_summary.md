# Oka's coherence theorem

> **Source.** Freitag, *freitag.pdf*, PDF pp. 31--35 (printed pp. 28--32), Theorem 10.3 and Lemma 10.4.

## Statement

Let $U\subseteq\mathbb C^n$ be open and let

\[
F:\mathcal O(U)^p\longrightarrow\mathcal O(U)^q
\]

be a holomorphic matrix. For every $a_0\in U$, there are a neighborhood
$V\subseteq U$ of $a_0$ and sections
$s^{(1)},\ldots,s^{(N)}\in\mathcal O(V)^p$ such that, for every $a\in V$,

\[
\ker F_a=\sum_{j=1}^N\mathcal O_a s^{(j)}_a.
\tag{1}
\]

Thus the sheaf of relations among finitely many holomorphic functions is
locally finitely generated.

## 1. Reduction to one row

The case $q=0$ is immediate, and it suffices to prove the theorem when
$q=1$. Indeed, suppose it is known for
target ranks $q-1$ and $1$, and write

\[
\alpha:\mathcal O^q\to\mathcal O^{q-1},\qquad
\beta:\mathcal O^q\to\mathcal O
\]

for the coordinate projections. Near a fixed point, choose
$A^{(1)},\ldots,A^{(m)}$ generating every stalk of $\ker(\alpha F)$, and let

\[
G:\mathcal O^m\to\mathcal O^p,\qquad
G(h)=\sum_{r=1}^m h_rA^{(r)}.
\]

After shrinking again, choose $B^{(1)},\ldots,B^{(\ell)}$ generating the
stalkwise kernel of the scalar row $L=\beta FG$. Then the sections
$G(B^{(s)})$ generate $\ker F$. In fact, they lie in $\ker(\alpha F)$ and
in $\ker(\beta F)$, hence in $\ker F$. Conversely, if $x\in\ker F_a$, then
$x=G_a(h)$ for some $h$, and $0=\beta F_a(x)=L_a(h)$; expressing $h$ in the
$B^{(s)}_a$ expresses $x$ in the $G(B^{(s)})_a$.

Induction on $q$ now reduces everything to the scalar case.

## 2. Oka's bounded-degree lemma

Put

\[
A=\mathbb C\{z_1,\ldots,z_{n-1}\},\qquad B=A\{w\},\qquad
P_{<m}=A[w]_{<m}.
\]

Let $F=(F_1,\ldots,F_p):B^p\to B$, where every $F_i\in P_{<d}$ and $F_1$
is monic. Set

\[
K=\ker F,\qquad K_m=K\cap P_{<m}^{\,p}.
\]

**Oka's lemma.** For every $m\ge 3d$,

\[
K=B K_m.
\tag{2}
\]

Here $BK_m$ denotes the $B$-span of $K_m$.

### The Weierstrass case

First suppose that $F_1$ is a Weierstrass polynomial. For $j\ge2$, put

\[
E^{(j)}=-F_je_1+F_1e_j\in K_d.
\]

Given $G\in K$, divide each coordinate by $F_1$:

\[
G=F_1A'+R,\qquad A'\in B^p,\quad R\in P_{<d}^{\,p}.
\]

Let $S=\sum_iA'_iF_i$ and $H=R+Se_1$. A coordinate calculation gives

\[
G=\sum_{j=2}^p A'_jE^{(j)}+H.
\tag{3}
\]

Moreover,

\[
F(H)=0,\qquad F_1S=-\sum_iF_iR_i\in P_{<2d}.
\tag{4}
\]

The second identity and uniqueness in Weierstrass division imply that $S$ is
a polynomial of degree $<2d$. Hence $H\in K_{2d}$, while every
$E^{(j)}\in K_d\subseteq K_{2d}$. Equation (3) proves

\[
K=BK_{2d}.
\tag{5}
\]

### The monic case

For a merely monic $F_1$, Weierstrass preparation gives $F_1=QU$, where $Q$
is Weierstrass and $U\in B^\times$. Since $F_1$ and $Q$ are polynomials,
uniqueness of division by $Q$ shows that $U\in A[w]$; moreover
$\deg Q,\deg U<d$.

Let $F'=(Q,F_2,\ldots,F_p)$, $K'=\ker F'$, and

\[
T(P_1,\ldots,P_p)=(UP_1,P_2,\ldots,P_p).
\]

Then $F'T=F$, so $T(K)=K'$. By (5), $K'=BK'_{2d}$. Multiplication by the unit
$U$ does not change the span, so $K'$ is also generated by the vectors $UR$,
$R\in K'_{2d}$. Their inverse images are

\[
T^{-1}(UR)=(R_1,UR_2,\ldots,UR_p)\in K_{3d}.
\]

Thus $K=BK_{3d}$, and $K_{3d}\subseteq K_m\subseteq K$ proves (2) for every
$m\ge3d$.

## 3. Induction on the dimension: the scalar case

Assume the theorem in dimension $n-1$, for all finite source and target
ranks, and consider a row

\[
F=(F_1,\ldots,F_p):\mathcal O(U)^p\to\mathcal O(U)
\]

near $a_0\in U\subseteq\mathbb C^n$. Translate $a_0$ to the origin. Any
$F_i$ whose germ is zero vanishes after shrinking; split off its coordinate,
with $e_i$ as a kernel generator. We may therefore assume that all remaining
germs are nonzero; if none remain, we are done.

Let $h_i$ be the first nonzero homogeneous term of $F_i$. Choose
$v\in\mathbb C^n$ outside the finitely many proper algebraic sets
$h_i(v)=0$, and make $v$ the last coordinate direction. The restriction of
each $F_i$ to the last coordinate line is then nonzero. Weierstrass
preparation and a common shrinking give

\[
F_i=Q_iU_i,
\]

where $Q_i$ is Weierstrass in $z_n$ and $U_i$ is a holomorphic unit. The
diagonal automorphism

\[
D(P_1,\ldots,P_p)=(U_1P_1,\ldots,U_pP_p)
\]

satisfies $QD=F$ and identifies their kernel stalks. It is therefore enough
to treat the row $Q=(Q_1,\ldots,Q_p)$.

Shrink to a product $V\times D_r\subseteq\mathbb C^{n-1}\times\mathbb C$,
and choose $d$ larger than every $z_n$-degree of the $Q_i$. Fix $m\ge3d$,
set $E_s=\mathcal O(V)[z_n]_{<s}$, and consider

\[
\Phi_m:E_m^p\longrightarrow E_{m+d},\qquad
\Phi_m(P)=\sum_iQ_iP_i.
\tag{6}
\]

Taking coefficients identifies (6) with a holomorphic matrix

\[
\mathcal O(V)^{mp}\longrightarrow\mathcal O(V)^{m+d}
\]

on the $(n-1)$-dimensional base. By the induction hypothesis, after
shrinking $V$ its kernel is generated stalkwise by finitely many polynomial
vectors

\[
R^{(1)},\ldots,R^{(N)}\in E_m^p.
\tag{7}
\]

Fix $a=(a',a_n)\in V\times D_r$ and put $w=z_n-a_n$. Translation from $z_n$
to $w$ preserves degree and monicity, and identifies the stalkwise kernel of
(6) at $a'$ with the bounded kernel $K_m(a)$ over
$A=\mathcal O_{a'}$. Thus the centered germs of (7) generate $K_m(a)$ over
$\mathcal O_{a'}$. Oka's lemma, applied in
$B=\mathcal O_{a'}\{w\}=\mathcal O_a$, gives

\[
\ker Q_a=\mathcal O_aK_m(a)
=\sum_{s=1}^N\mathcal O_aR^{(s)}_a.
\]

The same finite family works for every $a\in V\times D_r$. Transport it back
through $D^{-1}$ and the coordinate change, and restore the standard basis
vectors belonging to the zero columns. This proves the theorem for one row
in dimension $n$.

## 4. Completion of the proof

For $n=0$, the assertion is ordinary finite-dimensional linear algebra.
Assume it in dimension $n-1$ for every target rank. Section 3 proves the
one-row case in dimension $n$, and Section 1 then proves every finite target
rank. Induction on $n$ proves (1).

## Notes on the source

- In the diagram on printed p. 30, $\alpha$ must project to the first $q-1$
  coordinates and $\beta$ to the last coordinate. The scalar map is
  $\beta FG$, not $\beta A$.
- In the special Oka lemma, the conclusion is $H\in K_{2d}$.
- The $3d$ argument must first replace $R\in K'_{2d}$ by the equally
  generating vector $UR$ before applying $T^{-1}$. One cannot generally
  infer that an inverse-unit multiple of a polynomial is polynomial.
- The last factor of the polydisc on printed p. 32 is a complex disc $D_r$,
  not the real interval $(-r,r)$.
